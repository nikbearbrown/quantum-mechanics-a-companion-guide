## A. Conceptual foundations

### 1. Central potential and separation of variables

The hydrogen Hamiltonian is

H = −(ℏ²/2m)∇² − e²/(4πε₀r)

with the Coulomb potential V(r) depending only on r, not on θ or φ. This spherical symmetry is the mathematical license for separation of variables. Write the wave function as a product

ψ(r, θ, φ) = R(r) · Y(θ, φ)

and the time-independent Schrödinger equation Hψ = Eψ splits into a radial equation for R(r) and an angular equation for Y(θ, φ). The angular equation has the same form whenever V depends only on r — it is not specific to hydrogen. Its solutions are the spherical harmonics Y_ℓm(θ, φ), and the constants ℓ(ℓ+1) and m that label them are the orbital and magnetic quantum numbers.

What the companion adds beyond Griffiths Ch. 4.1: an explicit derivation showing why the centrifugal term ℏ²ℓ(ℓ+1)/(2mr²) appears in the radial equation as a consequence of the separation, not as an ansatz pulled from classical mechanics. The pop-sci book never mentions separation of variables.

**Source anchors:** Griffiths, *Introduction to Quantum Mechanics*, 3rd ed., §4.1 (separation in spherical coordinates), §4.2.1 (the angular equation). Schrödinger 1926, "Quantisierung als Eigenwertproblem (Dritte Mitteilung)," *Annalen der Physik* 80, 437–490 — the original wave-mechanical solution of hydrogen [verify exact issue/page].

### 2. Bohr model as a limiting case that got lucky

Bohr's 1913 model quantizes angular momentum as L = nℏ (he originally wrote it as L = nh/2π). This single postulate, plus Newton's law for circular motion under a Coulomb force, gives:

- Radii: r_n = n² a₀ where a₀ = 4πε₀ℏ²/(m_e e²) ≈ 0.529 Å (the Bohr radius)
- Energies: E_n = −13.6 eV / n² (the Rydberg formula written in eV; the Rydberg constant R_H = m_e e⁴/(8ε₀²h³c) was already known empirically from spectroscopy)

The Schrödinger solution gives the *same* energy levels E_n = −13.6 eV / n² for hydrogen. This is a coincidence with a deep reason — hydrogen has a hidden SO(4) symmetry generated by the Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector (a conserved vector that exists for any 1/r potential). Pauli exploited this symmetry in his 1926 paper to solve hydrogen algebraically, before Schrödinger published the wave-mechanical solution. The "extra" degeneracy among states of different ℓ at the same n (the n²-fold degeneracy ignoring spin) is the fingerprint of this symmetry. Most atoms do not share it; once you add a second electron, the symmetry is broken, and Bohr-style semi-classical quantization fails.

What the companion adds: name the coincidence honestly. The pop-sci book presents Bohr's model as a step toward the truth without ever saying that Bohr's quantization condition is wrong (electrons don't have definite circular orbits) — it just happens to predict the right *energies* for the one atom Bohr applied it to. Griffiths makes this point briefly; the companion expands it because students who only see Bohr's success leave with the wrong picture of what the model says.

**Source anchors:** Bohr 1913, "On the Constitution of Atoms and Molecules," *Philosophical Magazine* Series 6, vol. 26, pp. 1–25 (Part I), pp. 476–502 (Part II), pp. 857–875 (Part III). Pauli 1926, "Über das Wasserstoffspektrum vom Standpunkt der neuen Quantenmechanik," *Zeitschrift für Physik* 36, 336–363 (the SO(4) algebraic solution). Griffiths §4.2.2 for the wave-mechanical comparison.

### 3. Quantum numbers and orbitals

Four quantum numbers specify a hydrogen state:

- **n** (principal, n = 1, 2, 3, …) — sets the energy E_n = −13.6 eV / n²
- **ℓ** (orbital angular momentum, ℓ = 0, 1, …, n−1) — eigenvalue of L²: L² ψ = ℏ²ℓ(ℓ+1) ψ
- **m_ℓ** (magnetic, m_ℓ = −ℓ, …, +ℓ) — eigenvalue of L_z: L_z ψ = m_ℓ ℏ ψ
- **m_s** (spin projection, m_s = ±1/2) — eigenvalue of S_z: S_z ψ = m_s ℏ ψ

The spectroscopic letter labels (s, p, d, f) for ℓ = 0, 1, 2, 3 come from pre-quantum spectroscopy: sharp, principal, diffuse, fundamental — observed line series in alkali spectra. The labels stuck even though they describe nothing about the orbital shapes.

**Misconception to correct (pop-sci Ch. 5 — and most textbooks below the Griffiths level):** An orbital is not a "shape the electron traces out." The electron does not orbit. |ψ_nℓm(r,θ,φ)|² is a probability density. The familiar pictures — s orbitals as spheres, p orbitals as dumbbells, d orbitals as four-lobed clouds — are isocontours of |ψ|² (typically the surface enclosing some fixed probability, often 90%). They describe where the electron is *likely* to be found, not a path it follows.

This is the single most damaging picture in introductory chemistry. The companion shows what an orbital actually is by computing |ψ_100|² explicitly and plotting it.

**Source anchors:** Griffiths §4.2 for the full set of (n, ℓ, m) wave functions; §4.4.3 for spin. NIST Physical Reference Data, atomic spectra database (for the s/p/d/f spectroscopic origin) [verify URL].

### 4. Spin: intrinsic angular momentum with no classical analog

Spin is a quantum number with no classical limit. The "electron spinning on its axis" picture, taught uncritically in many high-school chemistry classes, gives wrong answers: if you compute the equatorial speed required for an electron (modeled as a sphere of classical radius r_e ≈ 2.8 × 10⁻¹⁵ m) to have angular momentum ℏ/2, you get a speed greater than c. The picture fails by orders of magnitude.

Spin enters the formalism axiomatically. Goudsmit and Uhlenbeck proposed it in 1925 to explain alkali doublet splittings; Pauli initially objected (correctly) to the classical-rotation picture, and developed the matrix formalism (the Pauli matrices, see Unit 7) the same year he formulated the exclusion principle. Dirac's 1928 relativistic wave equation derived spin-1/2 from the requirement of Lorentz invariance — spin is what you get when you do quantum mechanics consistently with special relativity, not a property you add by hand.

What the companion adds beyond Griffiths §4.4: a more explicit takedown of the classical-rotation picture, with the numerical estimate that exposes it. Griffiths mentions this in a footnote; the companion makes it a body paragraph because it is the single misconception students arrive with.

**Source anchors:** Uhlenbeck & Goudsmit 1925, "Ersetzung der Hypothese vom unmechanischen Zwang durch eine Forderung bezüglich des inneren Verhaltens jedes einzelnen Elektrons," *Die Naturwissenschaften* 13, 953–954. Dirac 1928, "The Quantum Theory of the Electron," *Proceedings of the Royal Society A* 117, 610–624.

### 5. Stern-Gerlach: the experiment that forced spin

Stern and Gerlach (1922) sent a beam of neutral silver atoms through an inhomogeneous magnetic field. Classically, atoms with magnetic moments at all orientations should produce a continuous smear on the detector. The experiment produced two discrete spots. Silver atoms have one unpaired s electron (closed-shell core plus a 5s¹ valence electron), so the splitting reflects the projection of that single electron's spin angular momentum onto the field axis.

Important historical note: in 1922 Stern and Gerlach interpreted the result in terms of the old-quantum-theory "space quantization" of orbital angular momentum. They were not testing for electron spin — spin had not been proposed. The correct interpretation (the splitting reflects S_z of the unpaired electron, not L_z) came after Uhlenbeck and Goudsmit in 1925. Many textbooks elide this.

**Source anchors:** Stern & Gerlach 1922, "Der experimentelle Nachweis der Richtungsquantelung im Magnetfeld," *Zeitschrift für Physik* 9, 349–352. Friedrich & Herschbach 2003, "Stern and Gerlach: How a Bad Cigar Helped Reorient Atomic Physics," *Physics Today* 56(12), 53–59 — a careful historical reconstruction including the 1922 misinterpretation.

### 6. Pauli exclusion principle

Full statement: no two electrons in a multi-electron atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers (n, ℓ, m_ℓ, m_s).

This is the statement to teach at this point in the course. The deeper version — that fermionic wave functions are antisymmetric under particle exchange, ψ(1,2) = −ψ(2,1), and the Pauli exclusion principle is a corollary — belongs in Unit 8 (Identical Particles). The companion makes this layering explicit so students see why the antisymmetry move is coming.

Consequences:
- Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy, two per orbital (one m_s = +1/2, one m_s = −1/2). Without the principle, every electron would collapse to the n=1, ℓ=0 state.
- The periodic table's row structure (2, 8, 8, 18, 18, 32, …) is exactly the count of (n, ℓ, m_ℓ, m_s) combinations available at each shell.
- "Why don't atoms collapse into each other?" is genuinely answered by Pauli; the pop-sci book's brick-wall analogy (in Ch. 5) is on the right track here, but says nothing about why.

**Source anchors:** Pauli 1925, "Über den Zusammenhang des Abschlusses der Elektronengruppen im Atom mit der Komplexstruktur der Spektren," *Zeitschrift für Physik* 31, 765–783. Pauli's Nobel lecture (1946) is also a good narrative source [verify URL on nobelprize.org].

## B. Domain examples and cases

### Example 1: The 1s state — solve the radial equation by hand

For n=1, ℓ=0, the radial equation reduces to a one-variable ODE whose ground-state solution is

R_10(r) = (2/a₀^(3/2)) e^(−r/a₀)

with Y_00 = 1/√(4π), giving the full wave function

ψ_100(r, θ, φ) = (1/√(π a₀³)) e^(−r/a₀)

This is the canonical worked example. The companion derives it from the separated radial equation, shows the normalization integral ∫₀^∞ |R|² r² dr = 1, and computes:

- **Most probable radius:** maximize the radial probability density P(r) = |R(r)|² r² = (4/a₀³) r² e^(−2r/a₀). Set dP/dr = 0: r_mp = a₀. The most probable radius equals the Bohr radius exactly. Bohr got the right *length scale*; he just got the meaning of that length scale wrong (it is not the radius of a circular orbit; it is the peak of a probability distribution).

- **Expectation value:** ⟨r⟩ = ∫₀^∞ r · |R(r)|² r² dr = (3/2) a₀. The average distance from the nucleus is 1.5 a₀, not a₀ — the distribution is asymmetric (skewed toward large r).

The gap between r_mp = a₀ and ⟨r⟩ = 1.5 a₀ is a teaching opportunity. It exposes the difference between "where the electron is most likely to be found" and "the average distance from the nucleus." These are not the same number for a skewed distribution. Many students arrive having conflated the two.

**Source anchors:** Griffiths §4.2 (Table 4.7 lists ψ_nℓm for the first several states; the 1s integrals are worked in Example 4.2 [verify exact problem number]).

### Example 2: 2s vs. 2p — same energy, different spatial distribution

n = 2 admits both ℓ = 0 (2s) and ℓ = 1 (2p, three values of m_ℓ). All four states have the same energy E_2 = −13.6/4 ≈ −3.4 eV. This is the SO(4) degeneracy. The 2s state has a radial node (a sphere where |ψ|² = 0); the 2p has an angular node (the xy-plane is a nodal plane for the m_ℓ = 0 state, written as p_z). Same energy, very different geometry. The companion shows the radial functions side by side and points out that "same energy" does not mean "same shape" — it means "same eigenvalue of H," which is one thing, not all things.

### Example 3 (failure case): the Bohr model applied to helium

If you take Bohr's quantization condition and apply it to helium (Z = 2, two electrons), you get the wrong answer. The naive prediction E ≈ −Z² · 13.6 · 2 = −108.8 eV is off because Bohr's model has no machinery for electron-electron repulsion. The actual ground-state energy of helium is approximately −79.0 eV (experimental, from ionization energies summed). The discrepancy is the electron-electron repulsion energy that Bohr's model cannot account for. This is the canonical failure that motivates: (a) approximation methods (Unit 9), and (b) the full antisymmetry machinery of Unit 8.

The lesson is general. Bohr's model worked for hydrogen because hydrogen has one electron and a hidden symmetry. It works nowhere else. Cite this case as a guard against the comforting story that classical mechanics "almost" works.

**Source anchors:** NIST Atomic Spectra Database for the helium ionization energies (24.587 eV first ionization, 54.418 eV second; sum ≈ 79.0 eV) [verify URL: physics.nist.gov/PhysRefData/ASD].

## D. Current state of the field

The Schrödinger solution of hydrogen is mature 1926 physics; the quantum-mechanical treatment has not changed in its essentials. What has changed:

- **Precision tests of QED.** Hydrogen spectroscopy is now sensitive enough to test quantum electrodynamics at the level of the Lamb shift (Lamb & Retherford 1947), hyperfine structure, and the proton charge radius. The "proton radius puzzle" — a discrepancy between proton radii inferred from muonic hydrogen (Pohl et al. 2010, *Nature* 466, 213–216) and from electron-hydrogen spectroscopy — was a significant open question through the 2010s. Recent measurements (Bezginov et al. 2019, *Science* 365, 1007–1012, electronic hydrogen 2S–2P) appear to resolve it in favor of the smaller (muonic) value [verify].
- **Rydberg atoms in quantum computing.** Highly excited hydrogen-like states (n ≈ 50–100) have large dipole moments and long lifetimes, making them useful as qubits. The chapter does not need to cover this in depth but can name it as a connection forward.
- **Antihydrogen.** The ALPHA collaboration at CERN has trapped antihydrogen and measured its 1S–2S transition; the spectrum matches hydrogen to the precision of current measurements (Ahmadi et al. 2018, *Nature* 557, 71–75 [verify]). This is a CPT test, not new chemistry, but it is a striking confirmation that Schrödinger's hydrogen really is hydrogen.

The textbook chapter should mention these as "where the story continues" rather than try to cover them.

## E. Teaching considerations

**Where students struggle (common patterns):**

1. The orbital picture. Almost every student arrives believing that an orbital is a path. The companion must reset this hard. Use the most-probable-radius vs. expectation-value comparison (Example 1) to break the path metaphor.

2. "Why are there 2 electrons per orbital?" Many texts just assert it. The honest answer is m_s = ±1/2 — there are two spin states, and the four-quantum-number version of Pauli says one per (n, ℓ, m_ℓ, m_s) combination. Spell this out.

3. The "spinning electron" picture. Students arrive convinced spin is rotation. The companion must include the numerical takedown (equatorial speed > c if you take the picture literally). Otherwise the picture persists through Unit 7.

4. Quantum number ranges. Students memorize "n = 1, 2, 3…; ℓ = 0 to n−1; m_ℓ = −ℓ to +ℓ" without seeing where the ranges come from. The ℓ < n constraint comes from the radial equation (R(r) must be normalizable); the |m_ℓ| ≤ ℓ constraint comes from L² ≥ L_z² (operator inequality). Worth at least naming both.

5. The Bohr model's success. Students often leave thinking Bohr "almost had it." The chapter should make clear what Bohr got right (energy levels for hydrogen, the Bohr radius as a length scale) and what he got wrong (the picture of definite circular orbits with definite radii). Both at once.

**Recommended pedagogical moves:**

- Open with the 1885 Balmer formula. Balmer wrote down λ = B · n²/(n²−4) for the visible hydrogen lines empirically; the formula worked but he could not say why. Bohr's 1913 model was the first explanation. Schrödinger's 1926 solution was the first explanation that did not require any unjustified postulates. This is a clean "puzzle and unfold" arc.
- Run the 1s calculation on the page in real time. Do not relegate it to an appendix. This is the chapter's earned deep-dive.
- Compare hydrogen orbital plots (s, p, d) side by side; emphasize that they are |ψ|² isocontours, not electron paths.
- End with the periodic table tie-in as the synthesis: this whole apparatus (n, ℓ, m_ℓ, m_s + Pauli) is why chemistry exists in the form it does.

**Exercises to embed:**
- Compute ⟨r⟩ for the 2s state and the 2p state. Compare. (They differ even though E_2s = E_2p; the 2s electron is on average closer to and farther from the nucleus than the 2p.)
- Show that the ψ_100 wave function is normalized: ∫|ψ|² d³r = 1 (worked spherical integral).
- Estimate the hydrogen ionization energy from the most-probable-radius and the Coulomb potential; show it gives the right order of magnitude. Compare to E_1 = −13.6 eV.
